        <h1>Young people 'would rather live without TV than mobiles or net'</h1>
        <p id="stand-first" class="stand-first-alone">Ofcom survey points to 'digital divide', with half of all parents thinking they know less about the internet than their children</p>
    </div>
</div>

                         <div id="content"><ul class="article-attributes"><li class="byline"> <a class="contributor" href="http://www.guardian.co.uk/profile/taraconlan"> Tara Conlan</a> </li> <li class="publication"> <a href="http://www.guardian.co.uk/">guardian.co.uk</a>, <time datetime="2011-04-19T14:30BST">Tuesday 19 April 2011 14.30 BST</time> </li> </ul> 
<div id="article-wrapper" data-global-auto-refresh-switch="on">
<div id="main-content-picture"><img src="http://static.guim.co.uk/sys-images/Media/Pix/pictures/2011/4/19/1303219761164/Just-23-of-16--to-24-year-007.jpg" width="460" height="276" alt="Just 23% of 16- to 24-year-olds now they would struggle without TV"/> <div class="caption">Just 23% of 16- to 24-year-olds now say they would struggle without TV. Photograph: Randy Faris/Corbis</div></div>
<div id="article-body-blocks"><p>For the first time, more young people say they would prefer to give up watching television than doing without their mobiles phones or the <a href="http://www.guardian.co.uk/technology/internet" title="More from guardian.co.uk on Internet">internet</a>, according to an Ofcom survey.</p><p/><p>Reflecting the rise of online viewing and competition from activities such as social networking, just 23% of 16- to 24-year-olds now say they would struggle without TV.</p><p/><p>Instead, 28% told the media regulator's survey that they would miss their mobile phone and 26% the internet.</p><p/><p>In another blow for traditional television viewing, although it remains the medium that would be missed the most for the UK as a whole, it has decreased from 50% in 2009 to 44% in 2010.</p><p/><p>Research published by the regulator for its <a href="http://stakeholders.ofcom.org.uk/market-data-research/media-literacy-pubs/" title="annual media literacy report">annual adult and children's media literacy reports</a> has also revealed that the "digital divide" between the generations continues, with half of all parents thinking they know less about the internet than their children.</p><p/><p>Some 54% of eight- to 15-year-olds who use the internet at home have a social networking profile on sites such as Facebook, up from 45% in 2009 &#8211; the same percentage as for adult users.</p><p/><p>Ofcom's survey for 2010 also found that 48% of parents with children aged five to 15 reckon they are less internet-savvy than their offspring, rising to 70% among parents of 12- to 15-year-olds.</p><p/><p>However, parents appear to be less concerned about how their children use the internet, with fewer setting parental controls, down from 43% in 2009 to 37%.</p><p/><p>At the same time, children are spending longer on the internet than ever before, with more of them using mobiles and games consoles to go online.</p><p/><p>About a fifth of five- to 15-year-olds own a smartphone, increasing to a third among 12- to 15-year-olds.</p><p/><p>And 40% of them now access the internet in their bedroom, up from 31% in 2009.</p><p/><p>Ofcom's annual study of just over 2,000 over-16s and a similar number of five- to 15-year-olds also found that 74% of households have the internet at home and the amount of time people are spending on it increased from 12.2 hours in 2009 to 14.2 hours last year.</p><p/><p>Interestingly, 12- to 15-year-olds now say they spend 15.6 hours a week on the internet, just below the 17.2 hours they spend watching television.</p><p/><p><em>&#8226;&#160;To contact the MediaGuardian news desk email editor@mediaguardian.co.uk or phone 020 3353 3857. For all other inquiries please call the main Guardian switchboard on 020 3353 2000. If you are writing a comment for publication, please mark clearly "for publication".</em></p><p><em>&#8226; To get the latest media news to your desktop or mobile, follow MediaGuardian on </em><a href="http://twitter.com/#!/mediaguardian" title="Twitter"><em>Twitter</em></a><em> and </em><a href="http://www.facebook.com/mediaguardian" title="Facebook"><em>Facebook</em></a></p></div>

        <h1>Meet Watson, the computer set to outsmart the champions of Jeopardy!</h1>
        <p id="stand-first" class="stand-first-alone">Watson, an IBM computer is to compete on the US gameshow Jeopardy! – and it will probably win</p>
    </div>
</div>

                         <div id="content"><ul class="article-attributes"><li class="byline"> <a class='contributor' href='http://www.guardian.co.uk/profile/tomlamont'> Tom Lamont</a> </li> <li class="publication"> <a href="http://observer.guardian.co.uk" >The Observer</a>, <time datetime="2011-02-06" >Sunday 6 February 2011</time> </li> </ul> 
<div id="article-wrapper" data-global-auto-refresh-switch="on">
<div id="main-content-picture"><img src="http://static.guim.co.uk/sys-images/Observer/Columnist/Columnists/2011/2/2/1296668837669/Jeopardy--IBM-Man-V.-Mach-007.jpg" width="460" height="276" alt=""Jeopardy!" & IBM Man V. Machine Press Conference" /> <div class="caption">Contestants Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter compete against Watson at a press conference last month in Yorktown Heights, New York. Photograph: Ben Hider/Getty Images</div></div>
<div id="article-body-blocks"><p>When we look back from a ravaged future on the war between man and machine, will a cheery gameshow take the blame for starting it all?</p><p>This month <em>Jeopardy!</em>, a US quiz show that has broadcast there for decades, will televise a contest between two of its past champions and a super-intelligent computer. Built by <a href="http://www.guardian.co.uk/technology/ibm" title="More from guardian.co.uk on IBM">IBM</a>, Watson, as the computer is known, can answer questions in a silky digital voice and knows a hell of a lot of trivia on everything from children's fiction to archaeology and the musical oeuvre of Maurice Chevalier.</p><p>Sounds fun. But in just about every apocalypse-scenario science fiction has given us (<em>The Terminator</em>, <em>The Matrix, </em>anything by Asimov or Dick) it's the creation of a fun-sounding robot that kickstarts humanity's woes. Man builds a sentient computer: it's friendly and here to serve. And the sentient computer plays nice for a time, all the while rooting around in the Pentagon's mainframe for those nuclear codes…</p><p>"That's science fiction," said <em>Jeopardy!</em> host Alex Trebek, posed a question about the likelihood of Watson going rogue and killing us all. If anything, said Trebek: "IBM sees Watson more like the computer in <em>Star Trek</em>" – able, in other words, to comprehend questions posed in conversational language and answer likewise. The idea is that it will also learn to understand tricksy human traits such as irony, jokes and puns.</p><p>Hence the <em>Jeopardy!</em> outing. The gameshow poses its questions in roundabout fashion: the quiz master reveals an answer ("Star of <em>The Terminator</em>") and the contestants buzz in with a question ("Who is Arnold Schwarzenegger?"). Watson needs to be clever to untwist the obtuse questions, all the while coping with Trebek's folksy conversation.</p><p>Crucially, according to IBM whizz David Gondek, Watson has the ability to learn, and so engineers have been feeding it with tens of thousands of books' worth of information. "Just like the other contestants, Watson has been studying up," said Gondek last month. He didn't mention whether Watson had yet been exposed to military textbooks, or karate manuals – but the machine had been let loose on reams of information culled from the internet, especially to analyse human interaction and better learn how people speak.</p><p>Though Watson's episode of <em>Jeopardy!</em> won't be broadcast in America until 14&#160;February, journalists and bloggers got a glimpse of him in action at a test event last month at IBM headquarters in Yorktown Heights, New York. The event featured a short, three-minute round of trivia questions. Watson, appearing on the set like an upended flatscreen TV emblazoned with a colourful animated avatar, was flanked by his human rivals, Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter. Ken and Brad had both triumphed on the show in the past, winning a combined $6.3m (£3.8m) through their exploits.</p><span class="inline embed embed-media">
	<iframe title="YouTube video player" width="460" height="369" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/12rNbGf2Wwo" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
	</span><p>They were creamed. <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?feature=player_detailpage&amp;v=12rNbGf2Wwo#t=26s" title="">Watson bossed things from the start</a>, buzzing in with answers on questions about the walls of Jericho and Agatha Christie before either man could get to his button. Watson answered in a mellifluous computerised voice – think Stephen Hawking with extra zing – and in a neat visual trick its screen avatar changed colour depending on how sure it was about each answer. It was 97% certain Agatha Christie had once taken part in an archeological dig in Syria, <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WFR3lOm_xhE&amp;feature=player_detailpage#t=55s" title="">and so glowed green</a>; whether Macaulay Culkin had appeared in <em>My&#160;Girl</em> proved more of a challenge and Watson, only 67% convinced, glowed yellow.</p><p>The machine is so powerful, IBM claims, that the average home computer would need two hours to deconstruct and answer live questions like these; Watson can do it in less than three seconds. Powered by 2,880 processor cores and using 15TB of ram, it's thus a cumbersome beast. The flatscreen was only a front for the cameras; the real work was done backstage where Watson's 10 racks of computer guts whirred away at up to 80 teraflops, taking up roughly the same amount of space as a room-size wardrobe.</p><p>Because of its size Watson currently resides behind thick glass at IBM's New York HQ, but the company is looking forward to a day when miniature Watsons will be commercially available. Future iterations, says Gondek, will be "smaller, use less energy… Everyone could have one." The humanitarian ideal – and ask any sci-fi writer, there's <em>always</em> a humanitarian ideal – is that the machine will one day be canny enough to aid busy doctors by diagnosing patients and assessing x-rays. No word about potential jobs at the Pentagon.</p><p>Instead, Watson awaits his American television debut. After such a dominant performance at the test event the machine looks certain to secure the most high-profile victory for artificial over organic intelligence since 1997, when Garry Kasparov was beaten at chess by another IBM-made computer, Deep Blue. Brad and Ken's best hope might lie in there being a lot of questions about Macaulay Culkin.</p><p>Although in truth it might be safer all round if Watson wins. During the test event, when Ken or Brad <em>finally</em> buzzed in to answer a question first, Watson's avatar <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WFR3lOm_xhE&amp;feature=player_detailpage#t=101s" title="">briefly flashed an angry orange</a>. It all happened in a split-second and nobody on set seemed to notice, but Brad later made a crack about being "afraid of Watson's progeny coming back from the future to kill me". For all our sakes, Brad, don't provoke it.</p></div>
